Plain-language guide

How a public comment actually works

Federal agencies and state commissions are legally required to consider comments from the public before they approve large infrastructure projects. They do not all carry the same weight — but together they shape what regulators write into the final environmental record, what they require as mitigation, and what becomes evidence in any future legal challenge.

A comment doesn't have to be long.

It needs to be specific. The most useful comments name the project and docket, state who you are and why you're affected, and raise concrete concerns regulators can investigate — water sources, wildlife, soil, air quality, safety distances, traffic, or whether the public benefit really justifies the harm.

You can comment without becoming a "party."

Filing a comment with FERC does not commit you to anything. To get the right to appeal a final decision in court, you (or an organization that represents you) must "intervene" — a separate, more formal step. Either way, both comments and interventions go into the public record.

Each project page walks you through it.

On every dossier you'll find a copy-ready sample comment, the FERC eComment link, your state legislator contacts, and printable scripts for calling or mailing. Use what's helpful. Personalize what you can. Your county-specific knowledge — what's downstream from your land, where your kids go to school, what your wells are pulling from — is exactly what regulators don't have on their own.

The summary

On April 1, 2026, the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission released a Draft Environmental Impact Statement for the Kosciusko Junction Pipeline Project, proposed by two Boardwalk Pipelines subsidiaries — Gulf South Pipeline Company and Texas Gas Transmission. Federal Register

The project would build approximately 111 miles of new 36-inch natural gas pipeline across nine Mississippi counties, plus an 8.1-mile Columbia Gulf Lateral, two brand-new compressor stations totaling more than 85,000 horsepower, modifications to an existing compressor station, and four new meter stations. MS Rising Coalition Motion

The applicants say it will deliver 1.175 billion cubic feet of gas per day in new firm transportation capacity — capacity that industry reporting ties directly to hyperscale power and data center load growth across the Southeast. PGJ Online

The total project cost stated in the Applicants' Resource Report 5 is $991,510,000 — a nearly billion-dollar federal authorization. Yet by FERC's own staff calculations, only 11 to 16 permanent jobs would remain in Mississippi after construction. Rep. Thompson Comments, Acc. 20260518-0003

FERC is accepting public comments on the Draft EIS only until 5:00 PM Eastern Time on May 25, 2026. After that window closes, the staff begins writing the Final EIS — and your ability to put concerns on the regulatory record narrows dramatically.

Source: Federal Register Vol. 91, No. 65, April 6, 2026 — Notice of Availability of Draft EIS

The new infrastructure, line by line

According to the Federal Register Notice of Availability for the Draft EIS, the proposal includes: FR Notice

  • Gulf South side:
    • A new 102.9-mile, 36-inch Kosciusko Junction (KJ) Pipeline
    • A new 8.1-mile, 36-inch Columbia Gulf Lateral
    • A new Holmes Compressor Station — 23,984 horsepower, single natural-gas-fired unit, located along the existing Greenville Lateral
    • A new Kosciusko Compressor Station — 61,293 horsepower across three natural-gas-fired units, located along the new KJ Pipeline
    • Two new natural-gas-fired compressor units totaling 24,615 horsepower added at the existing Isola Compressor Station
    • Four new meter stations: Columbia Gulf, Destin Check, SONAT, and Kosciusko
    • Four mainline valves, tie-ins, launcher/receiver facilities, and other appurtenances
  • Texas Gas side:
    • Abandonment by sale to Gulf South of the existing Greenville Lateral — three 36-inch pipelines totaling 97.74 miles, plus a 0.37-mile 20-inch line
    • Abandonment by sale of the existing Isola Compressor Station
    • A new 24,160-horsepower gas-fired compressor unit installed at the existing Greenville Compressor Station

The applicants are not just building one new pipeline. They are reorganizing the existing Boardwalk gas network in the Delta and central Mississippi, transferring assets between two affiliated companies, and adding enough new compression to drive a massive new flow of gas to wherever the contracts point.

Wildlife & habitat

The Draft EIS acknowledges the project crosses wetlands, streams, and forested habitat across nine counties. The Mississippi Rising Coalition's intervention names "streams, wetlands, and forest habitats along the Project's approximately 111.5-mile pipeline route" as one of the central environmental concerns the Commission's Draft EIS must address. MS Rising Motion §V.B.2

What is on the public record

The Draft EIS confirms that construction will disturb an estimated 1,468 acres of land along the new pipeline corridors, with approximately 667 acres kept permanently cleared for pipeline operation. The remaining disturbed area would be restored. FERC Scoping Notice, May 13, 2025

Roughly 51 percent of the planned new 111.5-mile pipeline would be co-located with existing pipeline or utility rights-of-way. The remaining 49 percent cuts through previously undisturbed or minimally disturbed land — adding new fragmentation to forests, fields, and wetlands. Scoping Notice

Construction right-of-way is 75 to 135 feet wide; permanent operational right-of-way is 50 feet wide, centered over the pipeline. Beyond the ROW itself, the project adds 5 contractor yards, 15 new permanent access roads, and 87 temporary access roads — additional land disturbance not always visible on the route map. DEIS Summary, April 2, 2026

The Draft EIS explicitly acknowledges that long-term and permanent environmental effects will occur on "some forested lands, including forested wetlands." FERC characterizes most other effects as "temporary or short-term during construction" — but the forested wetland losses are permanent. Midstream Calendar, April 6, 2026

Hundreds of forested wetlands will be permanently converted

A public comment filed by Emily Evans on April 28, 2026 confirms what is buried in the DEIS itself: the Kosciusko Junction Pipeline and Columbia Gulf Lateral will cross hundreds of palustrine forested wetlands (PFO). The DEIS states that the conversion of those forested wetlands to scrub-shrub (PSS) or emergent (PEM) wetlands "would likely be permanent." Evans Comment, Acc. 20260428-5046

Palustrine forested wetlands are not interchangeable with the wetlands FERC proposes to leave behind. They provide flood control, carbon sequestration, sediment removal, and habitat for species that cannot survive in open scrub or grass wetlands. Converting them is a permanent loss of ecosystem function — even when the acreage on paper stays the same — and fails the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers' "no net loss" standard. Evans Comment

The U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service Mississippi Ecological Services Field Office is a cooperating agency on the EIS, which means Section 7 Endangered Species Act consultation has been initiated. The U.S. Army Corps of Engineers is also a cooperating agency, confirming that Clean Water Act Section 404 permitting for wetland and waterbody crossings is required before construction. Scoping Notice §III

Water & soil

The 111-mile route crosses multiple major watersheds in the Mississippi Delta and central uplands. The Big Black River, Yockanookany, and Pearl River tributaries are all within the project corridor across Holmes, Attala, and Leake counties.

The state water permit is still missing

Before the project can proceed, the Mississippi Department of Environmental Quality (MDEQ) must issue a Section 401 water quality certification under the Clean Water Act. As of the Notice of Application in September 2025, that certification had not yet been issued. FERC Notice of Application, Sept. 30, 2025

The U.S. Army Corps of Engineers must also issue a Section 404 permit for any dredge or fill in wetlands and waterbodies. USACE sits as a cooperating agency on the EIS because the project will require this permit for the crossings. Scoping Notice

Why the Mississippi Delta crossing matters

The pipeline traverses Washington, Sunflower, Humphreys, and Holmes counties — the heart of the Mississippi Delta and home to some of the most extensive bottomland hardwood wetland and floodplain ecosystems remaining in the southeastern United States. These are the systems that filter sediment, buffer flooding, and support waterfowl, fisheries, and migratory species. FR DEIS Notice

Sourcing in progress — water specifics only in the full DEIS The full record will name:
  • Stream and waterbody crossings by construction method (open-cut vs. HDD vs. bore)
  • Specific named waterbodies — Big Black River, Yockanookany, Pearl River tributaries, and Delta drainage ways
  • Aquifers crossed and groundwater wells within the impact radius (Mississippi River Alluvial, Catahoula, Sparta)
  • Hydrostatic test water volume, source, and discharge location
  • Floodplain crossing acreage and erosion and sediment control commitments

Chemicals & emissions

The two new compressor stations — Holmes (23,984 hp) and Kosciusko (61,293 hp across three units) — plus the modified Isola station (24,615 hp added) and Greenville expansion (24,160 hp added) will combine to add more than 130,000 horsepower of natural-gas-fired combustion across Mississippi. Each compressor unit is a stationary source of nitrogen oxides (NOx), volatile organic compounds (VOCs), carbon monoxide, formaldehyde, and hazardous air pollutants. FR Notice

What burns at the compressor stations

All four compressor stations — new Kosciusko (3 units, 61,293 hp), new Holmes (1 unit, 23,984 hp), modified Isola (+24,615 hp), and expanded Greenville (+24,160 hp) — use natural-gas-fired turbine and compressor units, not electric. Each unit burns pipeline-quality natural gas as its operating fuel and emits NOx, carbon monoxide, VOCs, formaldehyde, and fine particulate matter. FR DEIS Notice

FERC's climate gray zone

The Draft EIS acknowledges climate change impacts from the project but explicitly does not characterize them as significant or insignificant. FERC concluded that all other effects would be "less than significant" with mitigation — but on greenhouse gases, the agency punted. Midstream Calendar, April 6, 2026

What 1.175 billion cubic feet a day means in carbon

The pipeline is designed to deliver 1.175 Bcf/day of natural gas — expandable to 1.58 Bcf/day — primarily for electric generation, industrial load, and AI-driven hyperscale data centers. The supply comes from the Haynesville, Utica/Marcellus, and Fayetteville shale basins. When that gas is burned downstream, the resulting CO₂ load is on top of the compressor combustion emissions counted in the DEIS. Boardwalk Project Page

Sourcing in progress — emissions tonnages from the full DEIS and air permit applications The DEIS Vol. 1 air-quality section and Vol. 2 air permit appendices contain:

  • Modeled ambient concentrations at the property line and at the nearest residences, schools, and churches

Workforce & wages

On May 18, 2026, U.S. Representative Bennie G. Thompson, ranking member of the House Committee on Homeland Security and the congressman representing four of the host counties (Washington, Sunflower, Humphreys, Holmes), filed formal comments on the DEIS that flag economic concerns FERC has not addressed. Rep. Thompson Comments, Acc. 20260518-0003

Up to 1,539 out-of-state workers on a Delta jobsite

The DEIS projects a peak construction workforce of approximately 1,790 workers. By FERC's own numbers, only up to 181 of those workers (15 percent at the high end) would be local. Between 80 and 95 percent — up to 1,539 workers — would come from outside the project area. After construction ends, only 11 to 16 permanent jobs remain. Thompson Comments § 4.7.3 citing DEIS

The four host counties in the Second Congressional District alone have more than 36,000 people in the civilian labor force with unemployment rates ranging from 8.8 percent in Holmes County to 15.8 percent in Humphreys County — every one of them well above the 6.0 percent statewide rate and the 5.2 percent national rate. The DEIS contemplates importing the work and exporting the wages. Thompson Comments citing Resource Report 5, Table 5.2-2

The DEIS does not say what anyone will be paid

The Applicants' own filings put total construction payroll at $55,773,802 — with approximately $5.58 million (10 percent) assumed to be distributed to local workers. Roughly 90 percent of construction wages would leave the host communities entirely. The DEIS does not specify job types, skill levels, pay rates, whether workers will receive employer-sponsored health insurance, or whether retirement benefits will be offered. Thompson Comments citing Resource Report 5 § 5.3.1

The healthcare ripple

Rep. Thompson warns that importing 1,500+ uninsured or underinsured workers into the Delta lands on a hospital system already stressed past breaking. Mississippi hospitals incurred approximately $600 million in uncompensated care in 2019, equal to 7.2 percent of total hospital operating costs — the third-highest share in the nation. As of the 2025 Chartis report, 55 percent (37 of 67) of Mississippi's rural hospitals are at risk of closure, and 34 percent are at immediate risk within 2–3 years. Center for MS Health Policy, 2023 · Chartis 2025 Rural Hospitals Report

The contrast: Decatur Lateral Project, Illinois

Rep. Thompson points to the Decatur Lateral Project in Illinois (Docket Nos. CP25-94-000) as the counter-example: there, the developer committed in writing that at least half the construction workforce would be drawn from the host community, with family-sustaining wages, employer-sponsored family health insurance, pension contributions, and approximately $47 million in labor income flowing directly into the local community. "The Kosciusko Junction Pipeline currently offers my constituents nothing comparable." Thompson Comments p. 5

Environmental justice

FERC removed the analysis. The data is in the record anyway.

The Applicants' own Resource Report 5 contains a census-block-group-level environmental justice analysis. It expressly finds that the Project will impact a total of 87 census block groups, and of those, 67 — 77 percent — are environmental justice communities. Thompson Comments citing Resource Report 5 § 5.2.5

FERC excluded that analysis from the DEIS. Section 1.3 of the DEIS states: "all Executive Orders pertaining to environmental justice were revoked in January 2025; therefore, an assessment of effects on environmental justice communities is not required." Rep. Thompson's response: "That is not a defensible reading of NEPA. The National Environmental Policy Act still requires a meaningful assessment of a project's effects on the human environment." Thompson Comments p. 4

Cultural & tribal concerns on the record

A federally recognized tribe is asking for more time

On May 13, 2026 — just twelve days before the public comment deadline — the Choctaw Nation of Oklahoma, through its Historic Preservation office, filed a formal letter with FERC stating: Accession 20260513-5055

"This project lies in our ancestral homelands and has the potential to affect numerous cultural resources… given the large amount of data and cultural resources affected, we request that an extension be granted for our review. We request that the Section 106 review be completed prior to issuing the final permit for construction of this project."

The letter was signed by Lindsey D. Bilyeu, Program Coordinator II for NHPA Compliance Review.

State Historic Preservation Office review is incomplete

Two days later, on May 15, 2026, the applicants themselves filed a weekly status report admitting that the Mississippi State Historic Preservation Office (SHPO) has not issued final comments. The revised Phase I Cultural Resources Survey is not scheduled to be submitted to MDAH and FERC until June 2026, with an anticipated concurrence in July 2026 — both of which fall after the May 25 public comment deadline. Accession 20260515-5118

One specific archaeological site — Site 22CK716 — remained under active follow-up consultation as of May 11, 2026, between the applicants' consultant Perennial Environmental Services and the Mississippi Department of Archives and History.

The public is being asked to comment on a Draft EIS while a federally recognized tribe has formally requested an extension and the State Historic Preservation Office has not yet issued final concurrence on the cultural-resource record. NEPA requires the agency to consider these consultations as part of its decision-making.

Why the process itself is a concern

The Lexington scoping meeting that nobody attended

On April 30, 2026, FERC held its public scoping meeting for the Kosciusko Junction Pipeline at the City Hall Multipurpose Building in Lexington, Mississippi (Holmes County) — ground zero for one of the two new compressor stations. The official transcript filed at FERC (Accession No. 20260514-4000) records the meeting in its entirety: "No one from the public showed up to make any comments. The meeting was closed at 6:30 p.m." Lexington Scoping Minutes, Acc. 20260514-4000

FERC will cite the absence of attendance as evidence that the community has no objections. The truer reading is that the comment period landed in planting season in one of the most underserved counties in the country, with minimal notice and no real outreach — then closed before the DEIS itself was filed. This is the public record FERC says counts as community engagement.

Three filings, three holes in the record

Three separate filings on the public record in mid-May 2026 — within days of each other — suggest a regulatory process that is not yet ready for a final environmental decision:

  1. FERC issued an Environmental Information Request on April 23, 2026 — meaning Commission staff themselves found gaps in the applicants' submission that required additional response. The applicants are providing weekly status reports as those gaps are worked through. Acc. 20260515-5118
  2. The Choctaw Nation of Oklahoma requested an extension on cultural resource consultation on May 13, 2026, twelve days before public comments close. Acc. 20260513-5055
  3. Mississippi Rising Coalition, a Mississippi non-profit, filed a motion for leave to intervene out of time on May 14, 2026, stating that "Movant became aware of the Project's significant environmental impacts on the communities, ecosystems, and waterways it represents only following the Commission's Notice of Intent to Prepare an EIS and the subsequent publication of the Draft EIS in April 2026, which revealed the full scope and nature of the Project's potential harms for the first time." Acc. 20260514-5033

Together these filings make the procedural argument simple: the public comment deadline should not close while the cultural-resource record is incomplete, tribal consultation is unfinished, and affected communities are still discovering the project's scope.

The Applicants' own Resource Report 5, Section 5.2.5, finds that 67 of the 87 census block groups affected by this project — 77 percent — are environmental justice communities. The DEIS expressly excluded that analysis, citing the January 2025 revocation of environmental justice Executive Orders. As Rep. Bennie G. Thompson noted in his May 18, 2026 comments (Accession 20260518-0003), this is not a defensible reading of NEPA. The National Environmental Policy Act independently requires a meaningful assessment of effects on the human environment. I ask that FERC incorporate Resource Report 5's census-block-group EJ analysis into the Final EIS. 7. WORKFORCE AND HEALTHCARE IMPACTS. The DEIS Section 4.7.3 projects a peak construction workforce of 1,790, with 80 to 95 percent (up to 1,539 workers) imported from outside the project area, and only 11 to 16 permanent jobs remaining after construction. The DEIS does not disclose wage rates, health insurance, or retirement benefits for these workers. Mississippi hospitals already absorb the third-highest uncompensated care burden in the country, and 55 percent of our rural hospitals are at risk of closure. The Final EIS must analyze the impact of importing 1,500+ potentially uninsured workers on local hospital safety nets and on local construction wages. 8. PERMANENT LOSS OF PALUSTRINE FORESTED WETLANDS. The DEIS itself states that conversion of palustrine forested (PFO) wetlands to scrub-shrub (PSS) or emergent (PEM) wetlands "would likely be permanent." Hundreds of PFO wetlands will be crossed. Restoring acreage on paper does not restore ecosystem function and does not meet the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers "no net loss" standard. Compensatory mitigation should focus on creation, restoration, enhancement, and preservation of PFO habitat — not on replacement with lower-function wetlands. 9. PUBLIC CONVENIENCE AND NECESSITY. Under Section 7 of the Natural Gas Act, the Commission must find that the project is required by the public convenience and necessity. The 1.175 Bcf/day capacity reported by industry trade press is tied to Southeast power and data-center demand — not to Mississippi consumers. The total project cost of $991.5 million (Resource Report 5) yields only 11 to 16 permanent Mississippi jobs by FERC's own numbers. I ask that the Commission disclose the precedent agreements supporting this capacity and weigh those identified customer needs against the environmental, public-health, and landowner costs imposed on the nine Mississippi counties the pipeline would cross. Thank you for considering these comments. I respectfully request that they be made part of the official record in Docket Nos. CP25-547-000 and CP25-549-000.
